Design Process

I used the Double Diamond approach in the development of ‚Äúsilhouette‚Äù to ‚Ä¶ at each phase of the design process:

  • Discover what the actual problem is through user interviews and secondary research.
  • Define user needs by filtering through all the information collected during the discovery stage and elaborating on it.
  • Develop a solution that meets user needs, get user input and redesign accordingly.
  • Deliver a minimum viable prototype (MVP) that offers enough tangibility to find out whether it solves the initial problem.

User Interviews

I conducted a user survey to gain insight on users' shopping habits. To reach a larger audience, I wrote a short, 10-question survey and posted it in different Slack and Discord groups, all within the University of Florida, to get a general idea of how they shopped for new clothes. I received 15 responses.

From there, I reached out to a few individuals from the surveys for a more in-depth analysis. With each person we had a conversation on how they view‚Ä¶ Some noticeable patterns from these interviews were:

Results:

  • 86% said they would rather buy new clothing online than in person. Solution: I need to create a mobile app.
  • Most users would rather buy online, rather than in person, due to convenience and greater availability. Solution: The app needs to make it easy to find pieces and streamline the buying process.
  • 80% claimed that popular culture (TV shows, movies, social media, etc.) was a source of style inspiration. Solution: The app should focus on staying up to date with trending shows and movies.
  • Every single user interviewed has been interested in buying at least one article of clothing that they saw in a movie or TV show. Solution: Users should be able to find specific pieces easily.

Highlights

Navigation Bar at the Bottom of the Screen
According to Fitts‚Äô Law, the farther away a target is and the smaller its size then the more difficult it is for the user to correctly land on that target. So, I designed the navigation to be at the bottom of the screen so it will be easier to tap. Additionally, I only put three large, labeled buttons (also, the most licked tapped buttons) on the navigation bar, and put the two lesser clicked buttons in opposite upper corners to ensure that the target button will be clicked correctly.

Item Pins
Each identified clothing item will have a pin on it. This pin will lead directly to the item‚Äôs page, where they can see details and have the option to buy it.

Favorite's Page
The favorites page has large, informative cards that are easy to read and select. The tabs can be navigated by either a swipe or tap. There‚Äôs, also, a search bar to find specific pieces more efficiently.

Final User Testing

To validate my proposed solutions, I asked five users to perform the following set of tasks using the prototype using the minimum number of clicks:

  1. Edit your personal information in silhouette. (5/5 completed successfully)
  2. Check your notifications from the past week. (5/5 completed successfully)
  3. To be announced. (5/5 completed successfully)
  4. Find out the fashion pieces in Nora Lum‚Äôs outfit. (4/5 completed successfully)
  5. Buy the ‚ÄúShort Camel Coat‚Äù from your favorites. (5/5 completed successfully)

Ultimately, all users managed to successfully complete four out of the five tasks. One user had some difficulty with the fourth task, as the instructions were, admittedly, not very clear; but they ultimately completed the task with a couple extra taps.

Reflections

Lessons Learned

Wider variance in user testing participants 👴🏾
Everyone involved in my first round of testing was an engineering student between the ages of 19 and 24, which was a huge red flag, because I was getting similar responses from everyone. When I invited younger and older users to test the product during the second round of testing, I saw the product through a different lens and had to come up with solutions that would help their problems.

My personas were wayyyy too similar 😭
This was my first case study and it was pretty obvious in hindsight that I was designing I product that I wanted and not what the user wanted. I should have used personas that didn't reflect my friends and I, but instead reflected a wider audience.

I‚Äôm making the MINIMUM viable product, not the maximum 🤕
I allocated two weeks of my time to accomplish the simple goal of ‚Äúdesigning a product that will get on-screen fashion into user‚Äôs closets.‚Äù I quickly realized that after all the user research and testing, I simply didn‚Äôt have the time to design and test all the other extra features I was thinking of implementing. Under the time constraints I put on myself, I used the design process to accomplish that specific goal and recorded my results.

Final Thoughts

The main thing that I learned from this project is that I need to design for the entire audience, not for people who think like me. I made assumptions, which ended up confusing some users and lead to them tapping around aimlessly trying to complete tasks. At the end of the day, user testing and the feedback I gained from it was crucial to making a seamless, straightforward shopping experience for the users.

If I had more time for this project, the next step I would take is to design more features for the app like I alluded to in the personas. I decided to cut the extra fluff because I felt it was ultimately unnecessary for the case study. The whole point of this project was to effectively utilize the double diamond design process while creating a product and to document the experience, not to push a money-making app into the world.

Overall, for the time I put aside to complete this project, I think it was a success. I used the design process to conceptualize an aesthetically pleasing product, that puts its users first and I tried made things as simple as possible for users to buy things to maximize both business goals and user experience.
