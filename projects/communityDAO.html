Overview

Introduction

communityDAO is a universal portal to participate in DAO voting. dApp lmao

This case study was inspired by Vy Alechnavicius’ “UX Ideation Challenge: Blockchain and Web3 Powered Future CX.” A common theme in Web3 projects so far has been a lack of consideration for human needs and the UX, so the goal of this project was to identify and design a potential user experience that blockchain technology could revolutionize.



For a more in-depth look at my process, continue scrolling through the full case study. Otherwise, click here to jump to my solution.

Project Specs

  • Project Type: Conceptual Case Study
  • Duration: 2 weeks
  • Team: Solo 🧍🏾
  • Tools: Miro, Figma, Photoshop
  • Industries: Web3, Blockchain, Smart Contracts
  • Strategies: User Research, UX/UI Design, Branding, Prototyping, Usability Testing

Design Process

I used the Double Diamond approach in the development of communityDAO to … at each phase of the design process:

  • Discover what the actual problem is through user interviews and secondary research.
  • Define user needs by filtering through all the information collected during the discovery stage and elaborating on it.
  • Develop a solution that meets user needs, get user input and redesign accordingly.
  • Deliver a minimum viable prototype (MVP) that offers enough tangibility to find out whether it solves the initial problem.

Discover

What's the issue?

Nonprofits face some serious trust issues: About a third of Americans don’t trust charitable groups to spend their funds well, and more than 60% of people globally don’t have faith that groups can accomplish their missions. But groups that perform well may be able to raise more money simply by becoming more transparent: People like having the opportunity to vet places themselves before making a contribution.
Source: Fast Company

As trust in nonprofits sinks to new lows, charitable organizations could face many threats to their ability to carry out their missions, including trouble raising cash, attracting top talent, and persuading Americans to take action on social, environmental, and other key issues.

Only slightly more than half of Americans trust nonprofits, according to the 2019 Edelman Trust Barometer, an annual study of institutions around the world. It found that only 52 percent of Americans have faith that nonprofits will “do what is right.” A recent survey by the Better Business Bureau brought more bad news: Seventy percent said trust is “essential” before making a donation, but fewer than 20 percent of Americans said they highly trust charities.
Source: philanthropy.com

So, how can we improve trust between organizations and donors?

User Interviews

I reached out on social media to friends, asking for participants in an interview regarding their experiences with buying insurance. I gathered 5 participants, all between the ages of 20 and 55, who I interviewed via Zoom and in person. Some noticeable patterns from these interviews were:

Results:

  • Most users have been somewhat skeptical about where charitable donations end up.
  • 60% of those interviewed thought that they should have an opinion in how their donations were spent in some organizations.
  • Every single user interviewed would appreciate transparency in how donations were spent.
  • 60% of users were familiar with the concept of a DAO.
  • Every user that was familiar with DAOs thought of them favorably.

OK, so WTF is a DAO?

A decentralized autonomous organization (DAO) is an entity with no central leadership. Decisions get made from the bottom-up, governed by a community organized around a specific set of rules enforced on a blockchain.

Being internet-native organizations, DAOs have several advantages over traditional organizations. One significant advantage of DAOs is the lack of trust needed between two parties. While a traditional organization requires a lot of trust in the people behind it — especially on behalf of investors — with DAOs, only the code needs to be trusted.

Trusting that code is easier to do as it’s publicly available and can be extensively tested before launch. Every action a DAO takes after being launched must be approved by the community and is completely transparent and verifiable.

Source: Coin Telegraph

Define

Personas

Based on the user research I set up two personas. I referred to them throughout the entire product development process. Each persona had a scenario that identified a realistic goal the user might have when working with this app. The information about each persona focused on its goals and frustrations with interacting with charitable organizations, which drastically affected my design decisions.

Problem Statements

Problems:

  • Donors want more financial transparency from organizations.
  • Charities and nonprofit organizations would like to promote more transparency.
  • DAO’s are relatively new to the general public, and most don’t understand how they work.

Proposed Solutions:

  • A platform that makes it simple to see how an organization’s donation are actually being distributed.
  • A platform that lets charitable organizations explain their proposals and get feedback from their communities.
  • A dApp (decentralized application) that simplifies the process of participating in DAO voting.

How might we create a platform that increases transparency between organizations and their donors using blockchain technology?

Develop

User Flow

The user flow is based on the job stories and focuses on the two main goals users want to achieve with "silhouette".

  • Find a specific fashion piece:
    "I want to buy the cute top that the character Jules Vaughn wore on the fourth episode of Euphoria."

  • Discovering new accessories:
    "I want to find new style inspiration and save pieces that I like."

Low Fidelity Wireframes

I sketched each iteration and added the elements and screens that were necessary to reach users’ goals, to quickly see which ideas worked best. I drew the sketches on blank index cards to build and interactive paper prototype and tested some user stories with four different individuals. Using paper prototypes enabled me to examine my ideas before putting everything in the daunting process of digitizing.

IMPROVEMENTS:

  • Improvement 1: Adding Filters
    A couple users pointed out the fact that all the placeholder text in the paper prototype were for female characters. I did not take into consideration that male users might want to filter results by gender to see things more applicable to them.
    Solution: I decided to add a filter button that allows you to narrow down results by gender.

  • Improvement 2: Change the word “garments” to “pieces”
    A couple users pointed out the fact that all the placeholder text in the paper prototype were for female characters. I did not take into consideration that male users might want to filter results by gender to see things more applicable to them.
    Solution: I decided to cut out needless confusion by changing my word choice to “pieces” as in “fashion pieces.”

  • Improvement 3: Icons Need Labels
    Users were asked to think aloud while completing the usability testing and multiple users said that they were “guessing” that the magnifying glass icon would bring you to the “discover” tab.
    Solution: I decided that the icons in the bottom navigation bar needed to have labels.

Mid Fidelity Wireframes

I turned my revised sketches into a black and white interactive prototype in Figma. Creating mid-fidelity wireframes helps me focus on the visual consistency and hierarchy before applying styles. I tested the prototype in-person and remotely.

The pandemic struck around this time, so I got my family to agree to be additional participants in this round of testing and this was extremely eye opening.

IMPROVEMENTS:

  • Improvement 1: Change the word “pieces” to “items”
    My two oldest participants didn’t recognize the that the term “pieces,” implied “fashion pieces.” This was important because I realized that I was designing for mainly a younger, college aged audience and alienating a huge chunk of the population for no reason (the older crowd and those who don’t speak English as their first language) which is terrible for business.
    Solution: I decided to cater to a wider audience by referring to singular pieces as “items.”

  • Improvement 2: Search bars on pages with a lot listed
    A couple users remarked on how long the favorite screen was and how hard it would be to find specific outfits and items after dozens were added. This problem could be eliminated pretty easily with one feature.
    Solution: I added search bars on the favorites page.

Style Guide

After a few more iterations, I designed the final screens with Figma. My aim was a clean, modern look that helps users fulfill their goals quickly. "silhouette" is a fun fashion app; therefore, I focused on the colors scheme to help transmit a friendly, yet elegant, vibe.

I also designed the product to keep the look fresh, diverse and modern.

Highlights

Navigation Bar at the Bottom of the Screen
According to Fitts’ Law, the farther away a target is and the smaller its size then the more difficult it is for the user to correctly land on that target. So, I designed the navigation to be at the bottom of the screen so it will be easier to tap. Additionally, I only put three large, labeled buttons (also, the most licked tapped buttons) on the navigation bar, and put the two lesser clicked buttons in opposite upper corners to ensure that the target button will be clicked correctly.

Item Pins
Each identified clothing item will have a pin on it. This pin will lead directly to the item’s page, where they can see details and have the option to buy it.

Favorite's Page
The favorites page has large, informative cards that are easy to read and select. The tabs can be navigated by either a swipe or tap. There’s, also, a search bar to find specific pieces more efficiently.

Final User Testing

To validate my proposed solutions, I asked five users to perform the following set of tasks using the prototype using the minimum number of clicks:

  1. Edit your personal information in silhouette. (5/5 completed successfully)
  2. Check your notifications from the past week. (5/5 completed successfully)
  3. To be announced. (5/5 completed successfully)
  4. Find out the fashion pieces in Nora Lum’s outfit. (4/5 completed successfully)
  5. Buy the “Short Camel Coat” from your favorites. (5/5 completed successfully)

Ultimately, all users managed to successfully complete four out of the five tasks. One user had some difficulty with the fourth task, as the instructions were, admittedly, not very clear; but they ultimately completed the task with a couple extra taps.

Reflections

Lessons Learned

Wider variance in user testing participants 👴🏾
Everyone involved in my first round of testing was an engineering student between the ages of 19 and 24, which was a huge red flag, because I was getting similar responses from everyone. When I invited younger and older users to test the product during the second round of testing, I saw the product through a different lens and had to come up with solutions that would help their problems.

My personas were wayyyy too similar 😭
This was my first case study and it was pretty obvious in hindsight that I was designing I product that I wanted and not what the user wanted. I should have used personas that didn't reflect my friends and I, but instead reflected a wider audience.

I’m making the MINIMUM viable product, not the maximum 🤕
I allocated two weeks of my time to accomplish the simple goal of “designing a product that will get on-screen fashion into user’s closets.” I quickly realized that after all the user research and testing, I simply didn’t have the time to design and test all the other extra features I was thinking of implementing. Under the time constraints I put on myself, I used the design process to accomplish that specific goal and recorded my results.

Final Thoughts

The main thing that I learned from this project is that I need to design for the entire audience, not for people who think like me. I made assumptions, which ended up confusing some users and lead to them tapping around aimlessly trying to complete tasks. At the end of the day, user testing and the feedback I gained from it was crucial to making a seamless, straightforward shopping experience for the users.

If I had more time for this project, the next step I would take is to design more features for the app like I alluded to in the personas. I decided to cut the extra fluff because I felt it was ultimately unnecessary for the case study. The whole point of this project was to effectively utilize the double diamond design process while creating a product and to document the experience, not to push a money-making app into the world.

Overall, for the time I put aside to complete this project, I think it was a success. I used the design process to conceptualize an aesthetically pleasing product, that puts its users first and I tried made things as simple as possible for users to buy things to maximize both business goals and user experience.
